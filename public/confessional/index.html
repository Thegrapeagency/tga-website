I

The Festival

19 Crimes does not want a seat at the wine table. It would rather kick the table over. A disruptive lifestyle brand that pours from tumblers instead of glasses and goes looking for the edges on purpose. So we did not take it to a polite tasting. We took it to the loudest field in the country.

Jera On Air is the biggest punk, hardcore and metalcore festival in the Netherlands, now in its 32nd edition, three days on a field in Ysselsteyn with around 15,000 people a day. Architects, The Offspring, Papa Roach and Rise Against on the bill. A young, loud, tattooed crowd with a low tolerance for anything corporate.

A rebellious brand deserves a rebellious room. Jera rewards anyone willing to do something strange and commit to it completely, which is exactly what 19 Crimes was built to do.

Edition #32 Punk · Hardcore · Metalcore 15,000 per day
II

The Concept

A gothic-Victorian confession booth in matte black and gold. Inside it, a Wine Priest hearing the festival sins of anyone brave, drunk or curious enough to step in.

Confess your crime, take your absolution, walk out with a wine in hand and a penance card that pulls you to the bar. The booth was the theatre. The bar was the point. This is how the ritual ran, from first contact to a full glass.

III

What we built

Not a table with a banner. A full experience, built piece by piece, from the booth on the ground to the app in the Priest's hand to the wine behind every bar on the terrain.

A · The Booth
A gothic booth that stopped people mid-stride

Matte black with the 19 Crimes crest in gold above both units. Red velvet at the confessional, the "Or Don't" campaign across the bar front, the nineteen festival crimes down the side panels. Parked next to the speciality beer bar, it read sharp and modern rather than dusty, and it pulled a crowd from the first afternoon.

B
The Wine Priest
A character, in role for three full days

Belgian actor Alain Rinckhout held the role from open to close, sinister but playful, never comedy and never breaking. He worked the queue as hard as the booth, and he was the gravity the whole activation turned around. People came for him and stayed for the wine.

C
The Nineteen Festival Crimes
Nineteen sins, written for this crowd

Nineteen festival sins printed across the panels under one line: each one punishable by confession. They gave people permission to confess and the prompt to do it, so nobody froze wondering what to say. Every line signed off with Treasury's brand and legal team.

D
The Confession Cards
A memento that walked straight back to the bar

Every confessor left with a vintage ticket card: a QR to the live site on the front, a handwritten penance and a bar discount on the back. A keepsake, a nudge to the digital experience and a reason to spend, all in one object.

E
The Branded Metal Cups
A reusable cup that carried the brand

Custom 19 Crimes metal tumblers, on brand and built to survive a festival. They put the logo in people's hands, leaned into the reusable angle this crowd cares about, and doubled as a keepsake worth keeping.

F
The Wine Priest App · Custom Built
We built the tool that captured every confession

No off-the-shelf tool logs a confession mid-festival, so we built one. The Priest records ten seconds, it transcribes, and an AI layer rewrites it into clean 19 Crimes tone of voice. Offline-first so festival signal never breaks it, and a featured flag for the ones worth posting.

The Live Confessions Site
Confessions, live and moderated, on the web

Confessions did not stay in the booth. A public page carried them in near real time as drifting vintage cards, every word pre-moderated so the brand kept control. The QR on each card sent people straight to it.

Behind the bar, the Meisjes van de Wijn sommeliers in 19 Crimes shirts and hoodies, pouring the range and keeping the queue moving. Wine people who know the wines, dressed as the brand.

VII

What we learned

An honest read on what to change for the next edition.

The one thing that fought us
Three days of extreme heat

Peaks near 38 degrees, a heatwave that made national news, pushed the crowd toward colder, lighter drinks and into the shade. Bar sales came in under a projection built on average festival conditions. The experience held up. The weather simply moved what people drank.

Recommendation
Give the range a hot-weather hero

Add shade and cooling at the bar, and lead with a chilled, lower-alcohol serve on hot days, a 19 Crimes spritz or a heavily iced pour. Turn a heatwave into a sales angle instead of a headwind.

Recommendation
Start the confessions before the gates open

Run the live confessions site and a "confess yours" call to action in the weeks before the festival, with a few giveaway tickets. Walk in with an audience already primed and a queue from hour one.

Recommendation
Everything here is a reusable asset

The booth, the app and the live confessions site were built to last, not thrown away after one weekend. The next 19 Crimes activation starts from a running start: less to build, lower cost, faster to the field.
